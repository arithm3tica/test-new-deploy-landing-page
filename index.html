Power In Numbers

Arithmetica is a decentralized platform for computational mathematics that democratizes the computation and data availability.

Start Contributing

Collaborative Computation

Mathematicians are able to define problems and simply distribute computation to volunteers. Similar distributed computing projects have existed such as Folding at Home, Search for Extraterrestrial Life (SETI), and the Search for Mersenne Primes. Unlike single use platforms Arithmetica provides a reusable framework allowing the computation of new and diverse problem sets.

Explore Data

Arithmetica data is published without restriction and is shared freely with the public. Computational mathematics data is often siloed, restricted, and unable to be resumed. Free decentralized access to this data is an important tenant and will provide mathematicians with empirical data to help prove theorems.

Share Knowledge

Scientific Journals are big business and limit access to a select few. Arithmetica will become a place for mathematicians to communicate, get feedback, and share their findings on the distributed web. This sharing of knowledge will do for the mathematical community what GitHub did open source software and inspire the next generation of mathematicians.

Why it's Important

"Mathematics is not a deductive science - that’s a cliche. When you try to prove a theorem, you don’t just list the hypotheses, and start to reason. What you do is trial and error, experimentation, guesswork. You want to find out what the facts are, and what you do is in that respect similar to what a laboratory technician does."

Mathematics

Odd Perfect Number Conjecture

Perfect numbers are positive integers that are the sum of their proper divisors. The smallest perfect number is 6, which is the sum of 1,2, and 3. Other perfect numbers include 28, 496, 8128. No one knows if there exists any odd perfect numbers.

© 2018 Arithmetica

Arithmetica represents a new form of off-chain scaling in the blockchain space by combining Ethereum, IPFS, libP2P, and Infura to utilize computational cycles donated by altruistic users. Distributed altruism is the hallmark feature of Arithmetica; it avoids the complications that can arise from incentivization, eliminates data silos, and enables computational collaboration.
